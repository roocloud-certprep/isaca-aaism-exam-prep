# AI Risk Identification Explained

You can only manage the risks you have actually identified. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM)** exam prep series explores **AI risk identification** in depth: why AI risk is always contextual, how the familiar risk management life cycle applies to AI, the concrete steps for surfacing AI risk, and a well-known top-ten list of security risks for language-model applications. By the end you will be able to catch problems hiding inside tools you already use and speak a common language with the business and the security team about what could go wrong.

## Why AI Risk Is Always Contextual

The foundational truth of AI risk identification is that **risk is contextual** — it never floats in a vacuum. To manage AI risk effectively, you must first understand the full context of how the AI is being used. Is it embedded across an entire hiring process? Is it sitting quietly at one stage of a digital supply chain? Or is it simply present whenever employees use everyday productivity tools? Each context shapes which threats matter, how serious they are, and which controls make sense.

That context is also a moving target. Early adopters of AI can be blindsided when a system suddenly produces harmful content, leaks private information, or gets attacked outright. As the technology evolves and as attackers evolve with it, the nature of the risk changes right along with them. This is why AI risk identification cannot be a one-off exercise; it must be continuous, just like the systems and threats it tracks.

## Applying the Risk Management Life Cycle to AI

To handle both the technical risks and the wider risks to human rights and reputation, the familiar **risk management life cycle** still applies. It gives a flexible, repeatable process that was never unique to AI, and that is the point — well-understood discipline, applied to a new and shifting subject. The first stage of that life cycle is identification, and for AI it begins in a very specific place: accurately inventorying where AI is actually used.

Why start there? Because people often have no idea AI is baked into the tools around them. Productivity suites, ticketing systems, marketing platforms, and developer tools quietly ship AI features that staff happily click through without ever flagging as "AI." Without an honest inventory, AI risk identification cannot even begin, because you cannot manage what you do not know you have.

### A Concrete Danger From Poor Separation

Consider a concrete example. Imagine a recruitment assistant that is not properly walled off from a public-facing support assistant. A clever attacker could ride that connection to pull staff members' personal data straight out of the system. Poor separation turns what looked like a small oversight — two helpful AI assistants sharing more plumbing than anyone realized — into a serious breach. The lesson is that AI risk lives in the wiring between systems just as much as in the systems themselves.

## The Five Steps for Identifying AI Risk

Once the principle of context is established, identifying AI risk follows a clear sequence of steps that any security manager can apply:

1. **Map out the mission and objectives** of the AI program. Without a clear sense of what the AI is meant to achieve, risk has no anchor.
2. **Determine which products and services** deliver that mission. This grounds the risk discussion in real business outputs rather than abstract technology.
3. **Identify the assets that are vital** to delivering those products and services — technology, processes, vendors, and data. Each of these is a potential source of risk.
4. **Establish an AI value chain** that links it all together, so dependencies become visible and gaps become obvious.
5. **Assess the impact AI could have**, working through loss-event scenarios that reveal exactly how each risk would affect your objectives.

This structure turns AI risk identification from a vague worry into a concrete, repeatable exercise. It also produces evidence that the work has been done, which matters when regulators, auditors, or executives ask how you arrived at your view of the risk.

## The Top-Ten Risks for Language-Model Applications

Because AI applications are software at their core, a respected security community publishes a widely used **top-ten list of the most important risks for language-model applications**. Many of these echo familiar cybersecurity threats, which is good news: existing security skills carry over more than you might expect. Working through this list gives you a ready checklist of what to watch for when assessing any LLM-based system.

### The First Four Highlights

* **Prompt injection** — crafted input hijacks the model's behavior, turning a helpful assistant into an obedient accomplice for an attacker.
* **Sensitive information disclosure** — personal data leaks out through the model, often in answers the model thought were perfectly innocent.
* **Supply chain risk** — tampered or outdated third-party components silently weaken the system from underneath.
* **Data and model poisoning** — the training data is deliberately corrupted so the resulting model behaves the way the attacker wants.

### The Remaining Risks on the List

* **Improper output handling** — unvalidated outputs flow into other systems and trigger downstream damage.
* **Excessive agency** — the model gains too much power to act on its own, well beyond what the designer intended.
* **System prompt leakage** — the hidden instructions guiding the model expose secrets to a curious user.
* **Vector and embedding weaknesses** — flaws in retrieval-based systems that let attackers manipulate what the model "remembers."
* **Misinformation** — confident hallucinations presented as fact, which users then act on.
* **Unbounded consumption** — excessive use that leads to denial of service, financial loss, or even theft of the model itself.

Each of these has an analogue in traditional security thinking, but each also has an AI-specific twist that makes it worth studying in its own right.

## Bringing AI Risk Identification All Together

The whole picture is straightforward. **AI risk is contextual**, so identification starts by understanding how and where AI is used and by inventorying it thoroughly. From there, a structured set of steps moves from **mission to impact** within the risk management life cycle, so nothing important slips through. And because AI is software, the recognized top-ten list of language-model risks — from prompt injection and poisoning to misinformation and unbounded consumption — gives you a ready checklist of what to watch for at the technical layer.

For the AAISM candidate, the practical instinct to develop is to never accept "we use AI" as a complete answer. The right questions are: *Where, exactly? In which products, at which stages, with which data, and connected to what else?* Until those are answered, the risk is invisible, and invisible risk cannot be managed.
