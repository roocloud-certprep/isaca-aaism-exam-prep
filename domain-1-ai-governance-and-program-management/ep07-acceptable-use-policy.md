# AI Acceptable Use Policy (AUP) Explained

The **AI Acceptable Use Policy** is the single document that tells your colleagues, in plain terms, what they are and are not allowed to do with AI. This episode of the **ISACA AAISM** exam prep series defines the AUP, explains why every organization using AI needs one, and walks through the practical steps to take **before** you sit down to write it. Knowing how to build one means you can protect the organization from people feeding confidential data into the wrong tool, and you can give staff the clarity they need to use AI confidently instead of fearfully.

## What an AI Acceptable Use Policy Is

An **AI acceptable use policy** spells out what the organization has decided is permitted and what is forbidden, right down to which types and classifications of data may be used to train or feed an AI tool. At its heart, it is a **communication tool**; its job is to set clear expectations for staff so nobody has to guess where the line is.

Think of it like the posted rules at a community swimming pool. The water is there for everyone to enjoy, but the rules about diving, lanes, and supervision keep people safe and the pool open. An AUP does the same for AI: it lets people use the technology productively while protecting them, the organization, and its customers from foreseeable harm.

What goes into a particular policy depends on how much AI is disrupting your particular industry — a financial services AUP looks very different from one in retail — but the **preparation steps** are universal. Skip the prep and you will write a policy that is either too restrictive to be followed or too vague to be enforced. The remainder of this article walks through the eight preparation steps in order.

## Step 1 — Understand the Technology

Make sure the organization genuinely understands the AI it wants to use. That means knowing the common **model types**, **where their data comes from**, their **built-in biases**, and their **limits**. This is not optional background reading — without that understanding you cannot judge what to permit and what to ban. The understanding, alongside your needs and risk tolerance, also helps you decide between a **public AI service** and a **private one kept inside your walls**. A public, internet-based tool has very different data-handling implications than a private model running on infrastructure you control.

## Step 2 — Assess Organizational Needs

Get clear on exactly **how** the organization intends to use AI, because that purpose shapes every clause in the policy. An AUP written for a company using AI only for marketing copy will read very differently from one written for a company using AI in regulated decisions like credit or hiring. This is also the moment to research the **legal and regulatory requirements** specific to your industry and your region. The same use case may be fine in one jurisdiction and prohibited in another.

## Step 3 — Conduct a Risk Assessment

The risk assessment must identify both:

* **Technical risks** — like unintended or wrong outputs.
* **Ethical risks** — like the spread of misleading information.

Covering both lenses prevents an AUP that only addresses obvious technical failures while ignoring the social harms AI can cause. A model that performs accurately on a benchmark can still produce harmful societal outcomes, and the AUP needs to anticipate that.

## Step 4 — Confirm the Purpose and Scope of Acceptable AI Use

Decide what the policy is **for** and exactly which **systems it covers**. State plainly, for instance, whether it applies only to **generative AI** or to all AI models. Scope ambiguity is one of the most common failures of homegrown policies — staff genuinely do not know whether a clause covers a particular tool, and the policy quietly stops working.

Be explicit about the boundaries and the intent, whether that is ensuring **ethical use**, meeting **legal standards**, or simply giving users **clarity**. A clearly stated intent makes the policy easier to interpret in edge cases.

## Step 5 — Examine Existing Technology and Information-Security Policies

Before writing something brand-new, check for overlapping rules you can **borrow, reuse, or use as a template**. Most organizations already have data classification policies, information security policies, acceptable use policies for internet and email, and vendor management policies. A great deal of an AI AUP can live as targeted extensions to those documents rather than a standalone manifesto. Reuse keeps the policy library coherent and reduces conflicting rules that staff have to reconcile in the moment.

## Step 6 — Engage Stakeholders in Developing the Policy

Giving every relevant group a voice makes the policy both **comprehensive** and **actually workable**. Stakeholders bring:

* **Technical expertise** to assess feasibility.
* **Ethical alignment** to anchor the policy in shared values.
* **Legal compliance checks** to test it against regulation.
* **Practical operational feedback** so the rules survive contact with daily work.
* **A shared hand in assessing risk** and defining the guiding principles for AI use.

An AUP written behind closed doors and dropped on staff usually fails, because the people meant to follow it have not had the chance to surface the edge cases that would break it.

## Step 7 — Adopt a Governance Framework

The governance framework answers the questions of **ownership**, **responsibility**, and **assurance**, and gives decision-making a consistent, transparent, and accountable structure. Without one, every contentious AUP question becomes a one-off political negotiation. With one, the answer flows from the framework: this is who decides, this is who is consulted, and this is how the decision is recorded and reviewed.

## Step 8 — Prepare for Internal and External Communication

A policy is a **living document**, not a one-time memo, so teams must be told whenever it is updated or changed. Customers and regulators, too, will want to understand your **intended uses of AI** along with your **ethics and privacy safeguards**, so plan for that communication from the outset. The strongest AUPs come paired with a published summary that customers and regulators can read, alongside an internal version with the operational detail. Treating the AUP as a one-time launch event rather than an ongoing communication channel is one of the most common ways policies become irrelevant within a year of being written.

## Putting It Together: From Prep Work to a Living Policy

Once the eight preparation steps are complete, the AUP itself becomes much easier to draft. The technology is understood, the needs and risks are documented, the scope is clear, existing policies have been mined for reusable language, stakeholders have shaped the rules, a governance framework holds the accountability, and a communication plan is ready. The AUP that emerges from this preparation is one staff can actually follow — and one a regulator or auditor can read with confidence that the organization knows what it is doing with AI.

## Key Takeaways

* An AI AUP defines permitted and prohibited uses of AI, especially around data, and exists primarily as a communication tool to set expectations for staff.
* Like swimming-pool rules, an AUP keeps the benefits of AI accessible while protecting the organization, staff, and customers from foreseeable harm.
* Eight preparation steps come before drafting: understand the technology, assess needs, run a risk assessment, confirm scope, reuse existing policies, engage stakeholders, adopt a governance framework, and plan communication.
* The risk assessment must cover both technical risks (wrong outputs) and ethical risks (misleading information).
* An AUP is a living document — internal and external audiences need ongoing communication every time it is updated.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is an AI Acceptable Use Policy?
An AI acceptable use policy spells out what the organization has decided is permitted and what is forbidden when using AI, right down to which types and classifications of data may be used to train or feed an AI tool. At its heart it is a **communication tool**; its job is to set clear expectations for staff so nobody has to guess where the line is.

### Why does every organization need an AI acceptable use policy?
An AUP protects the organization from people feeding confidential data into the wrong tool and gives staff the clarity they need to use AI confidently instead of fearfully. Think of it like the posted rules at a community swimming pool — the water is there for everyone to enjoy, but rules about diving, lanes, and supervision keep people safe and the pool open.

### What are the eight preparation steps before writing an AI AUP?
The eight steps are: **understand the technology** including model types, data sources, biases, and limits; **assess organizational needs** and research legal and regulatory requirements; **conduct a risk assessment** covering technical and ethical risks; **confirm the purpose and scope** of acceptable AI use; **examine existing technology and information security policies** for reusable rules; **engage stakeholders** in developing the policy; **adopt a governance framework**; and **prepare for internal and external communication**.

### What risks should an AI AUP risk assessment cover?
The risk assessment must identify both **technical risks** like unintended or wrong outputs from the AI system, and **ethical risks** like the spread of misleading information. Covering both lenses prevents an AUP that only addresses obvious technical failures while ignoring the social harms AI can cause.

### Why should an AI AUP be treated as a living document?
A policy is a living document, not a one-time memo, so teams must be told whenever it is updated or changed. Customers and regulators also want to understand intended uses of AI along with ethics and privacy safeguards, so the organization must plan for both internal and external communication from the outset of policy creation.
