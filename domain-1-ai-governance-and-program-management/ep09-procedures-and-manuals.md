# AI Procedures and Manuals: Documentation Best Practices

Procedures are the detailed, step-by-step instructions that turn a high-level policy into consistent daily action. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM)** exam prep series looks at how standard operating procedures apply to AI, why AI is not just another ordinary technology system, and how organizations can use AI itself to help write and improve their procedures. Knowing how to adapt procedures for AI helps you make sure data is handled safely and outputs stay trustworthy, and knowing how to use AI to draft procedures, carefully, can save your team enormous time without sacrificing quality.

## SOPs, Manuals, and Playbooks Already Exist — Don't Reinvent Them

**Standard operating procedures**, **manuals**, and **playbooks** are familiar tools in any solid technology and cybersecurity program, and many of your existing procedures will already apply to a new AI solution. Change management, deployment, incident response, access reviews — these well-worn documents do not vanish just because you introduce a model into the stack.

The trap to avoid is assuming AI is just another ordinary system. That assumption is comforting but wrong, because AI introduces dimensions that traditional IT procedures never had to address. Reuse what fits, but be prepared to extend and rewrite for everything else.

## Why AI Is Not Just Another System

### The Data Problem
The clearest reason AI is different is **data**. AI consumes enormous amounts of it, so your procedures must cover how data is collected, handled, and protected when training and validating a model, and how it is cleaned and standardized — because that directly shapes the quality of the output. A procedure that worked perfectly for a static application does not automatically cover a model that ingests millions of records and learns from them.

### Ethical and Human-Rights Dimensions
Procedures also have to stretch beyond the technology team. Security teams must build **ethical and human-rights considerations** into AI procedures, covering areas that older procedures never anticipated. Decisions made by AI can affect people in ways that demand explicit guardrails — guardrails that were never part of the original SOP library.

### Cross-Organization Reach
Crucially, AI procedures must reach across the whole organization, not just the technology department. They must stay aligned with the **acceptable use policy** and **responsible-AI principles** so that AI stays secure and trustworthy everywhere it is used. The marketing team prompting a chatbot needs guidance just as much as the data scientist training the model.

### Maturing Best Practices
Because best practices here are still maturing, teams can adapt their existing, proven procedures, always reflecting the organization's **risk appetite** and **regulatory landscape**. There is no perfect template waiting to be downloaded; the best path is to evolve what already works, tested against the specific risks and rules your organization faces.

## Using AI to Write Procedures and Manuals

Now for an interesting twist: organizations increasingly use AI to write the very procedures and manuals they need, across all areas, not just technology. **Generative AI** and **natural language processing** — which is the ability of software to read and understand human language — can automate much of this drafting work.

But it is not as simple as typing a one-line request and trusting the result. The old computing rule of **garbage in, garbage out** applies fully, so some preparation is essential before you even start.

## Preparing to Use AI for Procedure Drafting

That preparation has two main parts, and skipping either one tends to produce vague, generic, or simply wrong output.

### Define Scope and Stakeholder Inputs
First, **determine exactly which processes and procedures you need**, defining the scope of the request so prompts can be tailored and the output can be properly validated before it is shared. The security manager should make sure stakeholders know how to describe a process clearly enough for the tool to work with. A model cannot read your mind; it can only work from what you tell it.

### Analyze Existing Documents
Second, **use language-processing tools to analyze your existing documents**, pulling out key information, workflows, and keywords to create consistent formatting and wording. **Dictation tools** can even help capture prompts by voice, which is useful when subject-matter experts find it faster to talk through a process than to type it. This step grounds new procedures in established conventions instead of inventing a parallel style.

## Human Oversight: The Principle You Cannot Drop

One principle must never be dropped: **human oversight**. Generating procedures with AI usually takes a back-and-forth conversation to refine the result, and the output is only ever a starting point. Always circulate the draft for review and verify it with your standard practices.

This is not bureaucratic caution — it is risk management. AI-drafted procedures can sound polished while subtly misstating a regulatory requirement, omitting a critical safety step, or contradicting another policy. Only human review catches those gaps before they harden into approved documents.

## Continuous Improvement with AI

Beyond drafting, AI can keep helping over time. It can:

* Analyze **user feedback and sentiment** to spot pain points in procedures.
* Test a process through **trial-and-feedback learning**, simulating how it performs.
* Flag **anomalies or bottlenecks** so procedures can be continuously improved rather than left to drift.

Used this way, AI shifts from one-off authoring help to an ongoing quality engine, surfacing issues that a manual review cycle might miss for months.

## Bringing It All Together

Standard operating procedures and manuals apply to AI, but AI's heavy use of data and its ethical dimensions mean you cannot treat it as just another system. Procedures must span the whole organization in step with the acceptable use policy. AI can also help author and refine those procedures through generative and language-processing tools — but only with careful preparation and, above all, human review of everything it produces. Get this balance right, and you turn procedures from a paper exercise into a living, AI-aware system that genuinely keeps the organization safe.

## Key Takeaways

* Many existing SOPs, manuals, and playbooks apply to AI, but it is a trap to assume AI is just another ordinary system.
* AI procedures must cover data collection, handling, protection, cleaning, and standardization because data shapes output quality.
* Procedures must build in ethical and human-rights considerations and reach across the whole organization aligned with the acceptable use policy.
* Generative AI and NLP can draft procedures, but require defined scope, analysis of existing documents, and tailored prompts to avoid garbage-in-garbage-out.
* Human oversight is non-negotiable: AI-drafted procedures are starting points that must be reviewed, refined, and verified against standard practices, and AI can also drive continuous improvement.
