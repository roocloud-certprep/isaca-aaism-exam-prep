# AI Roles and Responsibilities: Governing Body, Stakeholders, and the AI Charter

When an organization builds or adopts artificial intelligence, the question "who does what?" decides whether the project succeeds or quietly drifts into trouble. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM)** exam prep series maps out the four broad families of AI roles, the special duties of the governing body, how to handle internal and external stakeholders, and three practical alignment tools — the **AI charter**, the **AI steering committee**, and the **provider versus deployer** distinction. Understanding all this lets you look at any AI project and immediately see who is accountable when something goes wrong, spot when a project is missing a key player like a compliance voice or a clear executive owner, and know exactly which person or committee to raise an AI concern with instead of watching it fall through the cracks.

## Why AI Governance Is a Team Sport

Effective AI governance is really a set of oversight mechanisms whose job is to manage risks like bias, weak information security, privacy violations, and outright misuse, while still leaving room for innovation and trust to grow. Good governance is never the work of one person. It pulls in a wide circle of people — from the engineers who build the systems, to the everyday users, to policymakers and ethicists — so the technology reflects the values of society rather than the blind spots of a single team.

The exact roles in any organization depend on its size and on how it adopts AI. A company that only buys ready-made AI from outside vendors may have no AI developers or operations staff at all. Think of a kitchen: a tiny cafe might have one person who cooks, serves, and orders supplies, while a large hotel has separate chefs, waiters, and purchasing managers. Roles scale with the operation, and AI is no different.

## The Four Families of AI Roles

### 1. Leadership and Strategy
These are the people who set the direction. **Executive managers** set the tone at the top, sign off on the AI strategy, and carry ultimate responsibility for how AI affects the organization and its stakeholders. The **chief AI officer** leads the overall adoption of AI and reports up to executive management — this can be a dedicated person or a duty folded into an existing senior role. Alongside them sits the **AI steering committee**, a cross-functional group that offers insight on strategy and flags opportunities and concerns from across the business.

### 2. Development and Operations
These roles usually live inside the technology group. **Development** builds the AI solutions to meet specific business needs and includes data scientists, engineers, researchers, and developers. **Operations** then deploy and maintain those solutions and the infrastructure that runs them. **Product management** sits in between, bridging the technical staff and end users so the solution is actually usable.

### 3. Users and Their Support
**End users** are typically employees or customers who actually use the AI in their daily work or life. **Human resources** supports employee users through job classification and the right training. **Customer service** supports customer users when they have questions or run into trouble. These roles are easy to overlook, but a tool nobody can use well delivers no value.

### 4. Governance and Oversight
These are the people who keep everything honest. The **governance committee** oversees the AI program and its policies and reports on performance. **Risk management** folds AI into the wider risk planning. **Information security and privacy** protect data and systems throughout the AI life cycle. **Internal audit** independently checks AI-related risk as part of its audit plan.

## The Governing Body: Accountability That Cannot Be Delegated

The governing body carries unique weight. It sets goals that reach beyond money to include the culture, values, and ethics of the organization, and it works through tools like policies, strategies, job descriptions, regulations, training, performance indicators, and executive communication. The crucial point is this: **the governing body is accountable for everything the organization does, and that accountability cannot be handed off**. So before adopting any new tool, technique, or technology — including AI — its members must weigh the consequences carefully.

The governing body also decides whether a use of AI fits its **risk appetite** — the amount and type of risk it is willing to accept. That appetite can shift quickly as circumstances change, so a proactive governance system gives the organization the ability to adjust, pause, or even cancel a project when needed. Importantly, the governing body is *not* responsible for the inner workings of the AI itself; its duty is to stay informed about both the possibilities and the dangers. One specific danger to avoid is **anthropomorphizing** the AI — wrongly crediting a machine with human qualities like thinking, judging, or moral reasoning. Treating a statistical tool as if it were a wise colleague is a recipe for misplaced trust.

Because members can be held legally accountable for harm an AI solution causes, they must regularly review and improve five things: **management** (policies, strategies, resource allocation, ethical codes), **supervision** (evaluating value, risk, and decision quality), **evaluation** of internal and external factors such as emerging threats and opportunities, **reporting** to show interested parties that AI is being governed responsibly, and **oversight or final approval of change management** whenever AI use shifts.

## Stakeholders: Mapping the Internal and External Circles

Stakeholders are the people affected by or interested in the AI, and it is essential to weigh both internal and external perspectives. Best practice follows five steps: **map** stakeholders to identify everyone relevant and their concerns, **prioritize** those concerns so the most pressing are handled first, **engage and communicate** by updating people regularly and responding to feedback, **build in ethical AI development** with clear frameworks, and **monitor and evaluate** the AI's impact over time.

### Internal Stakeholders
* **Senior leadership and the board** need to see AI aligns with strategy, delivers real value, manages risk, complies with the law, and honestly addresses fears of job displacement.
* **Employees** need training and reassurance that AI will support their work rather than disrupt it.
* **Technical teams** need to confirm the infrastructure is secure, the deployment fits the wider strategy, and the data is good enough in quality, quantity, and accessibility to support the system over the long term.

### External Stakeholders
* **Customers and end users** want AI that is easy to use, protects their data under privacy laws such as the **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)** and the **California Privacy Rights Act**, is clear about how it affects them, and is free from discrimination.
* **Partners and vendors** need systems that integrate smoothly, open communication, and shared ethical standards.
* **Regulators and policymakers** expect compliance, honest dialogue, and proactive reporting.
* **Wider society** expects AI that is socially responsible, inclusive of diverse and marginalized groups, mindful of energy use, and transparent enough to ease fears of misuse.

## Tool 1: The AI Charter

A **charter** is a guiding document that formally defines the scope, objectives, and work of an AI initiative so everyone shares the same expectations. Picture the blueprint and building permit you agree on before constructing a house — the charter plays that role for an AI project.

A complete charter:
* Names and describes the initiative.
* Sets specific, measurable objectives.
* Defines what is in scope and, just as importantly, what is **out of scope** to stop the project quietly ballooning.
* Identifies internal and external stakeholders.
* Lays out the governance structure — steering committee, sponsors, working groups, reporting lines, decision-making process, and accountability for change management.
* Sets a timeline with milestones and outlines resources and budget (people, technology, data).
* Records the main risks and their mitigation strategies.
* Defines how success will be measured using metrics like return on investment, accuracy, adoption rate, and performance indicators.
* Captures formal approval through the signatures of key decision-makers.

## Tool 2: The AI Steering Committee

The **AI steering committee** provides strategic oversight during and after the project. Its core job is to keep AI initiatives aligned with organizational goals and to make the big calls — changes to budget and scope, and how to handle risks and challenges. It also makes sure AI practices stay ethical and compliant by reviewing policies on AI use, data handling, and bias reduction.

For the committee to work, its membership must be cross-functional, drawing from every business line the AI touches. A typical committee has a **chairperson** who is a senior leader such as the chief information, technology, or data officer; **project sponsors** who fund or champion the work; **domain experts** from operations or marketing; **technical experts** such as data science and security leaders; and **compliance officers** who give legal and ethical guidance. The group meets monthly or quarterly, with extra meetings called when urgent decisions cannot wait.

## Tool 3: Are You a Provider, a Deployer, or Both?

The final piece is understanding your own role. Ask whether the enterprise is an **AI provider**, an **AI deployer**, or both. Under the European Union's **AI Act**:

* A **provider** is any person or entity that develops an AI solution or model and puts it on the market, whether for free or for a fee.
* A **deployer** is any person or entity that uses an AI system in a professional capacity.

Think of a vehicle manufacturer versus a taxi company that drives those vehicles for hire — each carries very different responsibilities, a theme explored further in the **shared responsibility model**.

## Key Takeaways

* AI roles fall into four families: leadership and strategy, development and operations, users and support, and governance and oversight.
* The governing body is accountable for everything the organization does — accountability cannot be delegated, and members must guard against anthropomorphizing AI.
* Stakeholder management follows five steps — map, prioritize, engage, embed ethics, and monitor — across internal and external groups.
* The AI charter formally defines scope, objectives, governance, timeline, risks, success metrics, and sign-off for any AI initiative.
* The AI steering committee provides cross-functional oversight, and every organization must classify itself as a provider, a deployer, or both under the EU AI Act.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the four families of AI roles in an organization?
The four families are **leadership and strategy**, **development and operations**, **users and their support**, and **governance and oversight**. Leadership sets direction through executives, the chief AI officer, and the steering committee. Development and operations build and maintain solutions. Users plus HR and customer service support them. Governance and oversight includes the governance committee, risk management, information security, privacy, and internal audit.

### What is the role of the governing body in AI governance?
The governing body sets goals beyond money to include culture, values, and ethics, and is accountable for everything the organization does — accountability that cannot be handed off. It decides whether a use of AI fits the organization's risk appetite, stays informed about possibilities and dangers, avoids anthropomorphizing the AI, and regularly reviews management, supervision, evaluation, reporting, and oversight of change.

### What does an AI charter include?
An AI charter names and describes the initiative, sets specific measurable objectives, defines what is in and out of scope, identifies internal and external stakeholders, lays out the governance structure and reporting lines, sets a timeline with milestones, outlines resources and budget, records main risks and mitigations, defines success metrics like ROI and accuracy, and captures formal sign-off through signatures.

### What is an AI steering committee and who sits on it?
The AI steering committee is a cross-functional group that keeps AI initiatives aligned with organizational goals, makes big calls on budget, scope, risks, and ethics, and reviews policies on AI use, data handling, and bias. A typical committee is chaired by a senior leader such as the CIO, CTO, or CDO and includes project sponsors, domain experts, technical experts, and compliance officers, meeting monthly or quarterly.

### What is the difference between an AI provider and an AI deployer?
Under the European Union AI Act, a **provider** is any person or entity that develops an AI solution or model and puts it on the market, whether for free or for a fee. A **deployer** is any person or entity that uses an AI system in a professional capacity. The same organization can be both. The distinction matters because providers and deployers carry very different responsibilities under the shared responsibility model.
