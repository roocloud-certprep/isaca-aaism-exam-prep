# AI Business Cases: Scope, Cost-Benefit Analysis, and ROI

A solid business case is your best defence against wasting money on AI that looks exciting but solves nothing. This episode of the **ISACA AAISM** exam prep series walks through why every AI proposal deserves a documented business case, what that business case must contain, how to run a cost-benefit analysis, and how to think about return on investment in a realistic way. The payoff is concrete — a structured way to compare costs against benefits before you commit, and the language to set honest expectations with leadership so a slow start is not mistaken for failure.

## What an AI Business Case Must Contain

Any proposed AI solution should come with a **documented business case** whose job is to justify the investment. At a minimum, it should spell out five things:

1. **The problem** the AI is meant to solve.
2. **How the solution will solve it.**
3. **The expected return on investment**, based on a cost-benefit analysis.
4. **The risks** and how they will be handled.
5. **A detailed implementation plan.**

Writing this down matters more than it might seem. There is enormous excitement about what AI can do, and that excitement can sweep an organization into adopting a tool with no clear purpose. Without a business case tied to a genuine need, the project is far more likely to fail.

## Identifying the Right Problem

The first real step is identifying the right problem. This usually involves an internal committee, employee feedback, or qualified outside advisers, focusing on things like **operational inefficiency** or **frequent human error**. The discipline of naming the actual problem keeps the project honest, because nothing exposes a hype-driven proposal faster than asking it to point at the specific business pain it removes.

Once a problem is on the table, the organization has to make sure the chosen AI solution is **appropriate, feasible, and actually able to deliver**. A well-established solution removes a lot of uncertainty, because its costs and benefits are already well understood from real experience. Building something brand-new on an unproven, fast-changing concept does the opposite — it loads the project with high expectations and no clear path to delivering them. Whenever possible, prefer the proven solution over the novel one, even if the novel one looks more exciting.

## The Cost-Benefit Analysis: Three Cost Categories

A credible AI cost-benefit analysis examines three kinds of cost:

* **Implementation cost** — covers development, licensing, and training staff. These are the up-front investments to get the system live.
* **Operational cost** — covers ongoing maintenance, energy use, and any new salaries. AI infrastructure is rarely cheap to run, and energy consumption in particular can be significant.
* **Risk and compliance cost** — covers things like privacy protection and managing the organization's reputation. These costs are often forgotten in early calculations and discovered painfully later.

A common mistake is to focus only on implementation costs and ignore operational and compliance costs, which builds an unrealistically rosy picture. The discipline of separating all three forces a more honest projection.

## The Benefits Side: What You Are Buying

Those costs then have to be weighed against the expected benefits, which should tie directly back to the original problem. Three benefits show up again and again:

* **Efficiency and automation** — freeing people from repetitive tasks for higher-value work.
* **Better decision-making** — drawing insight from large amounts of quantifiable data.
* **Cost reduction** — lowering labor and error-related expenses across a process.

Note how each benefit maps to a measurable outcome: hours saved, decision quality improved, dollars reduced. If your business case lists benefits but offers no way to measure them, leadership has no way to judge success later.

## Return on Investment: Setting Honest Expectations

Return on investment simply means **what you get back compared to what you put in**. Industry surveys suggest that most organizations adopting generative AI do report a positive return, and often within the first several months of putting it into production. That is encouraging news for any executive weighing the move.

But there is a catch worth respecting. Early on, the return can look low, because the upfront costs are high and people are still learning the new tool. That is why you set reasonable expectations from the start. Increased revenue and reduced costs are the easiest gains to measure, but you should also count **human factors**, like happier employees and a better customer experience. A narrow ROI calculation that ignores those softer gains will systematically underrate the value of well-designed AI.

## How the Business Case Drives the AAISM Mindset

For an AI security manager preparing for AAISM, the business case is not just a finance document — it is the **anchor for governance**. Every later decision — which controls to apply, which vendors to vet, how much oversight to layer on, how to communicate to stakeholders — flows from the problem, scope, and risks defined here. A vague business case produces a vague program. A specific, measurable, risk-aware business case produces a defensible one. That is why the AAISM domain spends real time on this seemingly mundane document: it is the place where strategy becomes accountable.

## Key Takeaways

* Every AI proposal needs a documented business case that names the problem, the solution, the expected ROI, the risks, and the implementation plan.
* Identify the right problem first — operational inefficiency or frequent human error — and prefer well-established solutions over unproven, fast-changing ones.
* A credible cost-benefit analysis covers three cost categories: implementation, operational, and risk and compliance.
* Three benefits show up repeatedly: efficiency and automation, better decision-making, and cost reduction — and each must be measurable.
* ROI on AI is usually positive within months but starts low; count human factors like employee morale and customer experience alongside hard financial gains.
