# AI Standards and Frameworks: COBIT, Four Pillars, Laws, and Regulations

The rulebook your AI program is judged against has three layers that are easy to confuse: **voluntary standards and frameworks** that guide good practice, **broad governance frameworks like COBIT** that stretch across the AI life cycle, and **binding laws and regulations** that carry real penalties. This episode of the **ISACA AAISM** exam prep series sorts them out, walks through how a recognized framework maps to AI, and looks at where today's rules still leave dangerous gaps. Knowing this helps you choose a credible framework instead of inventing one from scratch, spot early whether a planned use of AI might breach a regulation and trigger heavy fines, and advise your organization on which markets and rules apply — especially if it operates across borders.

## Voluntary AI Standards and Frameworks

Standards and frameworks are structured sets of guidance that help organizations with governance, compliance, and day-to-day operations. AI-specific ones are still fairly new, shaped by a mix of academic research and industry input, and are usually published by standard-setting bodies backed by governments or industries. The key thing to remember is that most of these are **voluntary** — you adopt them to prove you are following recognized good practice.

### Headline AI Standards
* **ISO/IEC 23053** — describes a generic AI system that uses machine learning.
* **ISO/IEC 42001** — a voluntary management-system standard focused on ethical use, transparency, and accountability. It spells out how to establish, run, and keep improving an AI management system.
* **IEEE 7000** — gives developers a process for addressing ethical concerns, like privacy and fairness, while a system is still being designed.

### Wider Frameworks Worth Knowing
* **OECD AI Principles** — recommendations adopted across many countries, built around transparency, inclusiveness, sustainability, and accountability.
* **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)** — from the United States National Institute of Standards and Technology, it helps organizations identify, assess, and reduce AI risk.
* **Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework** — helps organizations deploy AI responsibly.

Beyond these, the international standards community has a whole growing family covering AI terminology, trustworthiness, and bias, so there is rarely a need to start from a blank page.

## COBIT Applied to the AI Life Cycle

**COBIT** is a broad and well-established framework for the governance and management of information and technology. Its main goal is to align technology with business objectives, manage risk, and make the best use of resources, with a strong emphasis on accountability. The neat thing is that COBIT can be stretched to cover AI by weaving its five domains through every stage of the AI life cycle, from design to retirement.

### Design — Evaluate, Direct, and Monitor
Helps leaders judge AI's potential impact, point resources in the right direction, and track performance against strategy.

### Development — Align, Plan, and Organize
Helps define management structures and set policies for data governance, security, and compliance, while planning resources and tackling bias and vulnerabilities.

### Deployment — Build, Acquire, and Implement
Governs how an AI system is integrated, tested, and validated, and handles change management so new tools do not disrupt existing systems.

### Operations — Deliver, Service, and Support
Once the system is live, this domain covers daily operations, service levels, and protection against cyber threats.

### Monitoring — Monitor, Evaluate, and Assess
Drives continuous checking against performance, compliance, and risk goals, so models can be refined as regulations evolve.

## The Four Pillars Framework from the Cloud Security Alliance

A second useful tool is the **Four Pillars framework** from the Cloud Security Alliance (CSA):

* **Pillar 1 — Model Card.** A transparent overview of a machine-learning model, including its objectives, training data, capabilities, limitations, and performance.
* **Pillar 2 — Data Sheet.** Describes the dataset used to train the model — how it was created, what it contains, its intended use, and any biases or ethical concerns.
* **Pillar 3 — Risk Card.** Systematically identifies and analyzes risks tied to a model, noting issues seen during development or deployment and the fixes planned.
* **Pillar 4 — Scenario Planning.** Imagines situations where the model could be misused or malfunction, surfacing unforeseen risks and shaping mitigation strategies.

Picture a continuous loop: model cards and data sheets describe the model, risk cards feed scenario-planning exercises, and the results flow back into risk management, so the whole cycle keeps learning.

## AI Laws and Regulations: From Voluntary Guidance to Binding Rules

Laws and regulations move us from voluntary guidance to binding rules. They give governments and their agencies the power to oversee AI. They are relatively new and largely untested in court, and many are still in draft form. The important consequence is that breaking them can bring fines or other penalties, both for organizations that build AI and for those that simply use it.

Many use a **risk-based approach**, sorting AI into tiers and applying stricter rules to higher-risk uses. The European Union's **AI Act** is the flagship example, with four tiers from **prohibited** down to **minimal risk**, transparency duties for things like chatbots and deepfakes, and penalties that can reach tens of millions of euros or a large slice of global revenue. Similar laws are emerging in Brazil, South Korea, several United States states such as Colorado, California, and Texas, and at the federal level — each with its own scope, penalties, and effective dates.

## Compliance Considerations for AI Systems

When it comes to staying compliant — especially with systems built on large language models — the biggest risk lives in the information people feed in. Four considerations belong at the front of mind:

1. **Confidentiality** — users can accidentally expose trade secrets or intellectual property when they paste sensitive data into a tool.
2. **Privacy** — protecting personal data **by design** and running a **privacy impact assessment** before an AI solution goes live.
3. **Intellectual property** — the copyright status of AI-generated content is still being debated, and using copyrighted material to train models can create legal exposure.
4. **Employee rights** — ensuring AI used in hiring or staffing stays fair, non-discriminatory, and within labor law.

## Where the Rules Still Leave Dangerous Gaps

Even as rules multiply, real gaps remain. Because countries do not coordinate well, rules often conflict or fail to line up, which makes life hard for organizations operating across borders and pushes up compliance costs. There are gaps by sector too: **healthcare** has moved quickly because patient safety and privacy are critical, while **finance and education** lag behind on fairness, transparency, and bias.

But the most damaging gap is often inside the organization itself, where no one clearly **owns** AI governance, so AI drifts out of step with the company's wider strategy and policies. Identifying that owner is one of the highest-leverage governance fixes available.

## Key Takeaways

* Voluntary standards like ISO/IEC 42001, ISO/IEC 23053, IEEE 7000, the OECD AI Principles, and the NIST AI RMF give recognized good practice for AI governance.
* COBIT stretches across the full AI life cycle through its five domains — Evaluate Direct and Monitor, Align Plan and Organize, Build Acquire and Implement, Deliver Service and Support, and Monitor Evaluate and Assess.
* The CSA Four Pillars framework — model card, data sheet, risk card, and scenario planning — creates a continuous learning loop around AI risk.
* Binding laws like the EU AI Act take a risk-based approach with tiered rules and very steep penalties; similar laws are emerging globally.
* Compliance pivots on confidentiality, privacy, intellectual property, and employee rights, and the worst gap is often the absence of a clear internal owner for AI governance.
