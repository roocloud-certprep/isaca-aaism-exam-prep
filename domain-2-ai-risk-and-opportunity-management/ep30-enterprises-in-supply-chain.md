# Enterprises in the AI Supply Chain: Roles Explained

No organization stands alone, and AI makes that truth louder than ever. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM)** exam prep series looks at why every enterprise depends on third parties, the risks those relationships introduce, and the crucial distinction between being an **AI provider** and an **AI deployer**. Your responsibilities and your legal liability depend heavily on which role you play in the AI supply chain, so knowing the difference lets you understand exactly what you are accountable for, what you can expect from your vendors, and where the lines of responsibility actually fall.

## No Enterprise Stands Alone

Let's start with a simple truth: no organization operates entirely on its own. From shared infrastructure to service providers and business partners, every enterprise relies on third parties, and those relationships bring their own risks. AI does not remove that dependence — it amplifies it, because AI tools, models, data, and infrastructure are increasingly sourced from outside the four walls of the consuming enterprise.

## Seven Faces of Third-Party Risk

Picture the many ways a supply relationship can fail. There is **operational risk** like production delays, **financial risk** like a supplier going bankrupt, **cybersecurity risk** like a partner's data breach, **compliance and ethics risk**, **geopolitical risk** from sanctions or conflict, **environmental risk** from natural disasters, and **reputational risk** when a supplier behaves badly. AI does not remove these; it adds new vendors and reshapes existing relationships. The audit and security manager's job is therefore to fold AI-specific concerns into a third-party risk picture that already had to track all seven of these dimensions.

## Context Sets Control and Liability

A key idea is **context**. How much control you have depends on where the AI sits, ranging from a system you built entirely in-house, where you hold the most control, to one supplied wholly by a third party, where you hold the least. That position shapes your response strategy and can even shift legal liability from one party to another. An enterprise that fine-tunes an open model on its own infrastructure has very different obligations from one that simply embeds a hosted assistant into its website, and the contract — and the law — recognizes that difference.

## The Central Distinction: Provider vs. Deployer

This leads to the central distinction: the **AI provider** versus the **AI deployer**. A **provider** is the entity that supplies the AI solution, while a **deployer** is the entity that uses it. Their duties differ sharply, and almost every regulatory and contractual question that arises in an AI engagement eventually comes back to which of these two hats the party is wearing.

### Provider Duties Under the EU AI Act

Under the European Union's AI Act, the provider carries the heavier compliance load:

* Ensuring high-risk systems meet legal requirements
* Running conformity assessments
* Maintaining technical documentation
* Monitoring performance
* Reporting serious incidents to authorities

That weight reflects the fact that providers shape what the system is, how it was trained, and what it can do. They are best placed to attest to its quality and to fix it when something goes wrong at the model level.

### Deployer Duties Under the EU AI Act

The deployer's duties are different in nature:

* Using the system as directed
* Assigning qualified people to oversee it
* Controlling what data is fed in
* Monitoring its responses and reporting issues back
* Being transparent with people that AI is involved
* Keeping logs for an appropriate period

Deployers may not have built the model, but they decide where it lives, who it talks to, and what data it sees — so the law assigns them the duties of stewardship, oversight, and honest disclosure.

## The Wider Cast: NIST AI Actors Across the Life Cycle

It helps to know that risk frameworks broaden this picture further. Rather than just two roles, a framework like the **NIST** one describes a whole cast of **AI actors** across the life cycle, from designers, data scientists, and domain experts at the design stage, through developers and integrators, to operators, evaluators, auditors, and the governance leaders and boards who provide oversight. The takeaway is that responsibility is shared across many hands, so knowing precisely where you stand in that chain is the foundation for managing your slice of the risk. For AAISM candidates this matters because exam questions often hinge not on whether *someone* should have acted, but on which specific actor should have, and at which stage.

## Bringing It All Together

Every enterprise depends on third parties, and those relationships carry **operational, financial, cybersecurity, compliance, geopolitical, environmental, and reputational risk** that AI reshapes. Your control and liability depend on context, from fully in-house to fully outsourced. And the core distinction is **provider versus deployer**, with providers carrying the heavier compliance burden and deployers responsible for proper use, oversight, and transparency, all within a wider cast of AI actors. You now know where an enterprise sits in the AI supply chain and what that means for accountability.
