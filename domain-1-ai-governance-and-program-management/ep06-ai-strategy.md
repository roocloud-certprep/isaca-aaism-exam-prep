# AI Strategy: Vision, Value Alignment, Build vs. Buy, and Vendors

A clear strategy is what stops an organization from buying AI tools at random and hoping for the best. This episode of the **ISACA AAISM** exam prep series defines what an AI strategy actually is, who should build it, the opportunities it unlocks, and the big practical choices that flow from it — including **value alignment**, the **build-versus-buy decision**, **vendor management**, and the **shared responsibility model** for off-the-shelf tools. The result is a framework you can use to argue for the right hosting choice, vet a vendor properly, and know who is on the hook when a bought-in tool misbehaves.

## What an AI Strategy Actually Is

Implementing AI is broadly similar to rolling out any other software, so a proper change-management process still applies — but AI carries unique business and risk considerations that you cannot ignore. An **AI strategy** is simply an organization's overall approach to adopting, developing, and governing AI, designed to capture the benefits while keeping risk under control.

Strategies exist at three levels:
* **National strategy** — aligns AI with a country's values, laws, and security.
* **Industry strategy** — promotes innovation and shared standards within a sector.
* **Corporate strategy** — hunts for competitive advantage while staying compliant.

A good strategy rests on agreed **principles**, usually the familiar ethical ideas of **explainability, fairness, transparency, and keeping humans at the center**. A well-known reference point is the set of principles from the **Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD)**, which come in two groups:

* **Five values-based principles** for trustworthy, human-centric AI: inclusive growth, respect for human rights and the rule of law, transparency and explainability, robustness and safety, and accountability.
* **Five recommendations to governments**: investment in research, an inclusive AI ecosystem, sound governance, building human capacity, and international cooperation.

## Who Owns the Strategy

A strategy only works if the right people own it. It must be **accepted by senior management** to succeed, and it should be drafted by a **cross-functional team** so every part of the business has a voice. Reassuringly, deep technical expertise is not strictly required to *set* the strategy — an organization short on internal AI knowledge should simply bring in outside experts or consultants. Many organizations lean on an existing group, like an innovation committee, with members drawn from technology, risk, legal, operations, and finance.

## Five Opportunities an AI Strategy Targets

The whole point of a strategy is to spot opportunities, and AI offers several clear ones:

1. **Increased innovation** — helping the organization create distinctive products or business models.
2. **Richer data analysis** — turning huge amounts of data into timely, actionable insight.
3. **Improved customer service** — through personalization and automation.
4. **Automation of routine processes** — which cuts operating costs.
5. **Stronger, continuous monitoring** of risk and compliance.

But capturing these benefits rarely comes from simply switching on a tool. More often, it requires **redesigning core business processes**, and sometimes even inventing entirely new business models. That is the real challenge facing leaders, and it is why **strategy, not technology, is the hard part**.

## Vision and Value Alignment

A strategy also needs a **vision** rooted in the organization's mission. An AI vision tells both staff and outsiders what the organization intends to do with AI and why — the destination written at the top of a travel plan. Without it, every individual decision lacks a reference point and teams pull in different directions.

This leads naturally to **value alignment**, which means designing AI so it behaves in ways consistent with human values and ethics. Alignment is not universal; it differs from one organization and one country to the next, so developers must consider their specific users and the values those users hold.

Four elements shape value alignment:
* **Community** — adapting the AI over time using real feedback.
* **Ethical foundations** — respecting universal human values and different cultural traditions.
* **Legal compliance** — following the laws of each region.
* **Operational strategy** — engaging stakeholders and refining the system continuously.

Value alignment does not happen by accident; it has **enablers**: frameworks and guidance to steer development, human engagement to refine the system, organizational change to weave alignment into culture, and audits and assessments to confirm the AI keeps living up to its ethical commitments. Crucially, alignment must be checked across the **entire life cycle**, which only a strong governance process can deliver.

## Build vs. Buy: On-Premises, Cloud, or Off-the-Shelf

Every organization faces the build-or-buy decision. You can purchase ready-made commercial software, or develop your own and run it either on your own premises or in the cloud. Picture the difference between owning a private workshop and renting a fully serviced unit.

**Running AI on your own premises** gives more control, customization, and tighter command of data security, but it costs more in computing power, energy, and staff.

**Running it in the cloud** scales easily and lowers upfront cost, but you give up some control and risk being locked into one vendor or hit by unpredictable charges.

The cloud choice adds contractual wrinkles too. If your training data is covered by privacy laws, even the **physical location** of the hosting provider can matter, so vendor oversight becomes essential. To decide well, an enterprise should weigh six questions:

1. How total costs compare across options.
2. Whether it has the data, skill, and time to build something as good as it could buy.
3. The regulatory risk of each path.
4. Which option fits its current operating model.
5. The data-privacy challenges of each.
6. The danger of vendor lock-in.

## Managing AI Vendors

When vendors are part of the plan, they must be vetted at onboarding and watched throughout the relationship, following standard procurement plus a **risk assessment**. For AI specifically, that assessment should check three things:

* The vendor's **strategy and values align** with yours.
* The vendor **honors ethics** like transparency and explainability.
* The vendor **protects data security and privacy**.

For ongoing oversight, set clear **service level agreements (SLAs)** — the documented promises about performance — and review them regularly. Whoever oversees these vendors must understand the organization's AI vision well enough to judge AI-specific concerns, and their input should feed into how you measure return on investment.

## The Shared Responsibility Model for Off-the-Shelf AI

If you lack in-house expertise, partnering with a vendor or an industry coalition for secure AI may be the smartest route. An **off-the-shelf solution** is a prebuilt tool from a third party, and while it speeds up adoption, it blurs the boundaries of who owns what. Three risks stand out:

* **Accountability and ownership** — unclear duties for maintenance can hurt performance or compliance.
* **Ethical concerns** — it can be unclear who is to blame when the tool produces biased or wrong results.
* **Data security** — sharing data widens the attack surface.

The key rule is this: **defining those responsibilities falls entirely on the buying organization**, because it is usually your people and processes feeding data into the tool. You must spell out clearly what you own, what the provider owns, and what you share. That clarity is the difference between a vendor partnership that works and one that quietly accumulates risk.

## Key Takeaways

* An AI strategy is the governed approach to adopting and developing AI, set at national, industry, or corporate level, owned by senior management and a cross-functional team.
* The OECD provides ten principles in two groups — five values-based for trustworthy AI and five recommendations to governments.
* Value alignment shapes AI to human values through community, ethical foundations, legal compliance, and operational strategy, enabled by frameworks, human engagement, organizational change, and audits.
* The build-vs-buy decision balances on-premises control against cloud scalability across six questions including cost, data-privacy, regulatory risk, and vendor lock-in.
* Off-the-shelf AI demands a shared responsibility model — accountability, ethics, and data security must be clearly assigned, and that assignment is the buying organization's job.
