# AI Risk Frameworks: NIST AI RMF vs. EU AI Act

Two **AI risk frameworks** dominate the global conversation on how organizations should govern artificial intelligence, and confusing them is one of the most expensive mistakes a security leader can make. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM)** exam prep series compares the voluntary **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** with the binding **European Union AI Act**, then draws out the key differences between them. By the end you will be able to pick the right tool for your situation, comply where compliance is mandatory, and adopt best practice where it is voluntary, instead of confusing a legal obligation with a helpful guideline.

## Setting the Stage: A Crowded, Fast-Moving Field

Many bodies are racing to keep up with AI, from standards organizations to academic ethics centers to international institutions. Because their guidance is young, you must check regularly that you are following the current version of whichever framework you have adopted — yesterday's leading practice has a habit of being quietly superseded. Two frameworks stand out today, and they fall into two fundamentally different types: regulatory and voluntary.

A **regulatory framework**, like the European Union's AI Act, creates legally binding obligations. Breaking them brings penalties or operating restrictions, and those penalties can be severe. A **voluntary framework**, like the NIST framework, offers best practices with no legal force. Following it is wise; ignoring it is not unlawful. Telling these two types apart is essential to aligning your strategy correctly, because a control regime built for one will not necessarily satisfy the other.

## The NIST AI Risk Management Framework

The **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** is a resource for anyone designing, building, deploying, or using AI. It is voluntary, flexible, and industry-agnostic, which makes it attractive to organizations that need structure without being forced into rigid categories. It is organized around four core functions that work together.

### The Four Core Functions: Govern, Map, Measure, Manage

* **Govern** — set the risk policies, accountability, and culture that surround the AI program. Without this layer, the rest of the framework has nothing to anchor to.
* **Map** — identify the system's characteristics, risk areas, and impact zones in context. The "Map" function ensures risk is understood in the setting where the AI actually operates.
* **Measure** — develop metrics to monitor risk across the life cycle. What is not measured cannot be improved, and AI risk shifts often enough to demand active measurement.
* **Manage** — act on risk in proportion to its impact, choosing the right responses for the right risks.

These four functions create a shared language for good outcomes, and they break down into more specific categories and granular subcategories, much like the structure of the NIST cybersecurity framework. The familiarity of that pattern is one reason NIST adoption tends to be easy for organizations that already speak NIST cybersecurity fluently.

Notably, NIST stays flexible and industry-agnostic. It offers tools to assess risk without forcing systems into rigid categories, which suits organizations whose AI portfolios cross multiple sectors or evolve rapidly.

## The European Union's AI Act

Now the contrast. The **European Union's AI Act** is a regulation that takes a risk-based approach and reaches any entity developing, deploying, or using AI within the Union. It does not invite organizations to adopt good practices — it tells them what they must do. Where NIST asks, the Act commands.

### The Four AI Risk Categories Under the Act

The Act sorts AI into four categories, each with very different consequences:

* **Unacceptable risk** — prohibited outright. These applications are simply off-limits.
* **High risk** — demands strict oversight, including conformity assessments and rigorous documentation.
* **Limited risk** — mainly requires transparency, so users know they are interacting with AI.
* **Minimal risk** — largely unregulated, leaving everyday low-impact uses unburdened.

Two features make the Act especially powerful. First, organizations deploying **high-risk systems must run conformity assessments** and meet strict legal standards, while **prohibited systems are simply off-limits**. Second, and crucially, the Act applies **extraterritorially**. Even organizations outside the European Union must comply if their AI affects people inside it. That global reach is what turns the Act from a regional rulebook into a worldwide compliance issue.

## How the Two Frameworks Truly Differ

Once both frameworks are understood individually, the real value comes from seeing where they part company. There are three particularly important differences.

### Risk Classification

On risk classification, the Act prescribes **explicit, mandatory categories tied to law**, with the four-tier scheme described above. NIST, in contrast, recognizes a **flexible spectrum with no fixed thresholds**, letting organizations characterize risk in the way that best suits their context. One is a rigid taxonomy with legal teeth; the other is a flexible vocabulary for thinking clearly about risk.

### Roles and Responsibilities

On roles, the Act clearly separates **providers, deployers, and distributors** and assigns each tailored duties, so legal obligations attach to specific actors. NIST, by contrast, uses the broad umbrella term of **AI actors** without dividing responsibilities into formal legal roles. For compliance purposes, the Act's clarity is necessary; for strategy purposes, NIST's flexibility is often useful.

### Third-Party Diligence

On third-party diligence, the Act acknowledges its importance but gives limited direction. NIST offers **more detailed, granular guidance** for managing third-party risk, which is one of the areas where the voluntary framework is materially stronger than the regulation. An organization wanting both legal coverage and practical depth on third-party AI risk often ends up using both frameworks in tandem.

## Bringing the Frameworks All Together

The big picture is straightforward. AI risk frameworks come in two types: **binding regulation** and **voluntary best practice**. The **NIST AI Risk Management Framework** is voluntary and flexible, organized around **Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage**. The **European Union's AI Act** is binding and prescriptive, sorting AI into **unacceptable, high, limited, and minimal risk**, and applying even beyond the Union's borders. They differ most in how they classify risk, assign roles, and guide third-party diligence.

For the AAISM candidate, the practical instinct is to never treat these two frameworks as interchangeable. Match the framework to the situation: if a system touches the European Union, the Act sets the floor. If you also want disciplined, detailed risk practice — especially around third parties — the NIST framework complements it well. Telling the two apart, and choosing them deliberately, is exactly the kind of decision a senior AI security manager is paid to get right.

## Key Takeaways

* AI risk frameworks come in two distinct types: binding regulation (the EU AI Act) and voluntary best practice (the NIST AI RMF).
* The NIST AI Risk Management Framework is organized around four core functions — Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage — and stays flexible and industry-agnostic.

* The EU AI Act applies extraterritorially, so organizations outside the Union must comply if their AI affects people inside it.
