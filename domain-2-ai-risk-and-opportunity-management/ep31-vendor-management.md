# AI Vendor Management and Key Considerations

Most organizations will consume AI through vendors long before they build their own, so **AI vendor management** is where much of your real AI risk lives. This episode of the **ISACA Advanced in AI Security Management (AAISM)** exam prep series looks at the new risks AI features bring to vendor relationships, the warning signs that should rule a vendor out, and the business considerations to weigh when selecting an AI vendor. Knowing how to vet a vendor properly lets you avoid being blindsided by a hidden AI feature and helps you choose partners whose practices you can actually trust.

## The Quiet Danger: Vendors Quietly Adding AI

Let's start with a quiet danger. Existing vendor management programs need updating for AI, because many vendors are now adding AI features to their services **with or without telling you**. This introduces fresh risk on several fronts:

* **Business risk**, when a new feature disrupts your workflows without warning.
* **Privacy risk**, if you do not understand how the vendor trained its models.
* **Security risk**, from new and untested exposures.
* **Compliance risk**, if the vendor falls behind shifting regulations.

The fix is to require **advance notice of new features** and to involve your legal, compliance, privacy, and security teams early. A clause that obliges a vendor to disclose every meaningful AI addition before it goes live is one of the highest-leverage contract changes a security leader can make right now.

## Vetting Matters Most at the Start

Folding AI into vendor management should happen **before you grow too dependent on a vendor**, because by then losing that service would seriously disrupt the business. So vetting matters most at the start. The window for serious due diligence is short: once a critical workflow has been built around a particular AI service, the cost of switching providers can quickly exceed the cost of accepting the risk, and that asymmetry is exactly what attentive vendor management is designed to prevent.

## General Warning Signs That Apply to Every Vendor

Certain warning signs should weigh against any vendor, AI or not, but they hit harder with AI:

* **Financial instability**
* **Regulatory noncompliance**
* **Poor security practices**
* **Reputational baggage**
* **Weak operational resilience**

Each of these is a classic procurement red flag, and AI does not change the calculus — it intensifies it. A vendor that cannot keep its books in order, its regulators happy, or its incidents contained will not become more dependable simply because it has put a model in front of you.

## AI-Specific Red Flags

For AI specifically, there are deeper concerns that should give you pause:

* A **lack of transparency or explainability** in the model.
* **Unacceptable ethical risk**.
* A **high chance of privacy violations**.
* **Vendor lock-in**.
* **Immature technology** used where health or safety is at stake.
* **No risk management framework** that accounts for AI.
* An **inability to meet your security requirements**.
* **No way to prove the origin of generative content**.
* **Resistance to due diligence, auditing, or security assessments**.

Sometimes a vendor is high-risk but still chosen, because the risk can be **mitigated, transferred, or accepted**. Other times the vendor simply exceeds your risk appetite, and then the expected benefits no longer outweigh the danger. The AAISM mindset is to decide deliberately which of those two situations you are in — never to drift into a dependency without naming it.

## Business Considerations for Selecting an AI Vendor

When you do select an AI vendor, weigh a set of business considerations across several domains.

### Strategy and Value
Confirm **alignment with your business strategy**, so the AI delivers real value rather than being a one-off experiment. The technology should serve a defined business outcome, not the other way around.

### Security and Risk
**Scrutinize the vendor's risk management, cybersecurity, incident response**, and, for generative tools, their **ability to prove content origin**. Provenance evidence is becoming a standard expectation for generative AI, and a vendor that cannot meet it is a vendor that cannot help you defend against intellectual-property and disinformation claims later.

### Data and Intellectual Property
**Examine data governance and quality**, including who owns the data and intellectual property and where the training data came from. Murky training-data origins translate directly into murky output rights — and into the kind of legal exposure that takes years to unwind.

### Integration and Expertise
**Assess technical capability** and how easily the solution integrates with your systems, including legacy ones. **Evaluate the vendor's expertise** and their **commitment to support, maintenance, and knowledge transfer** so you are not stranded after go-live.

### Compliance
And **confirm compliance with all relevant laws, regulations, and ethical guidelines**. Weighing these carefully lets you capture the benefits while keeping risk within bounds.

## Bringing It All Together

AI reshapes vendor management, because vendors add AI features that bring business, privacy, security, and compliance risk, so vet them early before dependence sets in. **Warning signs** like financial instability and poor security rule a vendor out, and **AI-specific red flags** include opacity, ethical risk, lock-in, and resistance to assessment. Sound selection weighs **strategy, risk management, data governance, integration, expertise, and compliance** together. You now know how to manage AI vendors with a clear eye.

## Key Takeaways

* Vendors are quietly adding AI to their services, so vendor management programs must require advance notice and pull legal, compliance, privacy, and security in early.
* Vet AI vendors at the very start, because dependence makes later off-boarding far more painful than rigorous up-front diligence.
* General red flags — financial instability, regulatory noncompliance, poor security, reputational baggage, weak resilience — apply with extra force when AI is involved.
* AI-specific red flags include opacity, ethical risk, privacy violations, vendor lock-in, immature safety-critical tech, no AI risk framework, inability to meet security needs, no content-origin proof, and resistance to audits.
* Sound AI vendor selection weighs strategic alignment, risk management, data governance and IP, technical integration, vendor expertise, and full legal and ethical compliance.
